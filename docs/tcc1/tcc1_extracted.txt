1 INTRODUCTION
The purpose of this study is to investigate the impact of exchange rate
volatility on bilateral trade flows, with particular emphasis on the trading
relationship between the U.S. and its main partners, Canada, Mexico and Brazil,
which is its largest partner in Latin America (USITC, 2025).
Canada and Mexico trade with the USA through USMCA (United States-
Mexico-Canada Agreement). However, Brazil trades extensively without a full-
fledged free trading agreement like that (USTR, 2020). The comparison between
an advanced and a developing economy will enable us to detect nuances or
differences on the pattern of sensitiveness that foreign trade demonstrates when
reproducing exchange rate fluctuations, contributing to enhancing the already
well-established debate (AUBOIN; RUTA, 2011).
The period analyzed and tested will be from 2000 to 2025, with a
combination of historical data and forecasts in the determination of trends and
estimations of future effects. This study is also interested in how fine-grained the
data will be regarding monthly to semi-annual time slices, so as to accommodate
the changes of volatile unpredictable trade flows and exchange rate movements
(SHEN et al., 2021).
Apart from an aggregate trade perspective, the study will also offer sectoral
analysis to ascertain why different commodities and services may respond
asymmetrically to exchange rate fluctuations, offering a better understanding of
the heterogeneity of effects among sectors.
The proposed study will use state-of-the-art machine learning models
(Random Forests, LightGBM) to forecast the effect of such fluctuations and
compare them to traditional statistical methods such as ARIMA in terms of
predictive ability (SONG; CHEN, 2024). It will include in its analysis the exports
and imports of goods between the U.S. and the chosen countries, using as
explanatory independent variables, macroeconomic indicators such as GDP,
nominal and real exchange rates, volume indices of production, and inflation
(KALLIANIOTIS, 2022). In addition, trade relationships and the exchange rate

dynamics can be seriously affected by outlier events (such as global financial
crises and pandemics), therefore the influence of such extreme observations on
predictive models will be examined to see how well they hold up in these high-

research will follow a structured approach according to CRISP-DM, from business
understanding to deployment of the model (MARTÍNEZ‐PLUMED et al., 2019).
The primary question to be answered is: "What is the impact of exchange
rate variations on export and import flows between the United States and
Canada, Mexico, and Brazil, and which forecasting models are best for making
these forecasts?"
There will also be additional questions regarding: What are the horizons
where predictive power is most informative for assessing the impact of exchange
rate movements on international trade, and how does this predictive power vary
across different models? Which industries are more sensitive with respect to
exchange rate changes and how does this vary between developed countries
versus emerging economies? In addition, this study endeavors to explore the
asymmetric effects on exports and imports in reaction to currency appreciation
and depreciation with a view to finding differences in behavior. We will also study
the effect of the depreciation of the dollar on bilateral trade balances, examining
how this type of monetary adjustment affects American export prices in foreign
markets as well as import prices for American consumers. Moreover, it will
compare the forecasting ability of traditional econometric models with that of
machine learning models in forecasting non-stationary or strongly fluctuating time
series.
The general objective of this study is to assess the relative performance of
Random Forest and LightGBM models with respect to ARIMA model in
forecasting how continuously changing exchange rates affect bilateral trade flows
between the United States, Canada, Mexico, and Brazil with granular level
insights about sectoral movement and macroeconomic effects.
The specific objectives are:

b) To determine the impact of these swings across different time horizons;

2 THEORETICAL FRAMEWORK
This section presents the theoretical basis to understanding how exchange
volatility impacts international trade flows, and how predictive models are used to
assess these dynamics. To ensure these answers are well-founded, we will seek
support from the fields of economics and international relations, as well as from
the technological field.
2.1 Economic key concepts
Nominal exchange rate: The nominal exchange rate is the relative price of
two currencies and plays a major part in determining international
competitiveness due to its impact directly on the price of imports and exports
(KIM; LE, 2024). As stated by Kallianiotis (2022, p. 46), “Devaluation increases
the price of imports and reduces the price of exports; and due to the law of
demand, imports are falling and exports are increasing and the trade account is
improved”.
REER: Real Effective Exchange Rate (REER) is a broader-based
measure that represents the relative price of goods and services of a country with
respect to those of other countries in its trading partner, adjusted for differences
in prices while accounting for inflation rates (WAHAB, 2024). It thus offers a better
understanding and knowledge of an economy’s competitiveness vis-à-vis others
since it considers inflation differentials that would, otherwise, either erode or
magnify the impact of nominal exchange rate changes (MWANGE et al., 2022).
Export and Import: Imports and exports are key parts of international trade.
Imports represent the purchase of foreign goods or services, while exports are
the sales of domestic goods or services abroad. These flows are of key
importance for a country's trade balance and general economy (NOTZ;
ROSENKRANZ, 2021).
HS: The Harmonized Commodity Description and Coding System (HS) is
a multifunctional international nomenclature that offers a common language for
handling classifications by trade sectors, indispensable for recording trade flows
=== PAGE 11 ===
11
at more detailed levels (SCHMIDT; STEINGRESS, 2019). The granularity offered
by the HS allows an analysis of how product categories react differently to
exchange rate changes, yielding very important sector-specific heterogeneities
(SELLAMI et al., 2024).
2.2 Models for determining the exchange rate and choosing exchange rate
variables: USD/XXX rate and REER
Various theories have been developed to try to explain those factors
affecting the determination of exchange rates; it can be inappropriate to rely on
one model to predict, as stated by Mwange (2022, p. 71),
Even though exchange rates can be confusing, practically everyone
has to understand them to function in the modern global economy. As
the world gets smaller, there is a greater likelihood that we will have to
deal with the risks associated with the fact that there are many different
currencies in circulation around the globe and that these currencies will
have a direct impact on our world. We must be able to evaluate how
changes in exchange rates will impact our financial planning, corporate
strategies, governmental initiatives, and other life decisions in order to
take the necessary action (both financial and otherwise). Examining
various exchange rate theories can help us.
The analysis of the nominal exchange rate (USD/XXX) is based on
traditional methods in the literature, which involves the link between interest rates,
inflation and expectations of devaluation as proposed by the International Fisher
Effect (IFE), and the relationship that exists between domestic interest rates, on
one hand, and foreign rates on other hand, in accordance with Interest Rate
Parity (IRP) which explains how exchange rate is connected to equilibrium among
money supply as well as demand for it according to monetary approaches.
Another relevant theoretical framework is the balance of payments theory, which
considers the exchange rate as being determined by the general conditions of
foreign transactions and financial exchanges among countries (MWANGE et al.,
2022). An assessment based on the U.S. dollar's nominal exchange rate is
conspicuous by its superiority as an anchor currency to inform and shape prices
internationally even for trade with third countries that do not include the U.S.
directly (GOPINATH et al., 2020). As SPRING-RAGAIN, (2024, p. 4) states,
Leading currencies, such as the US dollar (USD) and the euro (EUR),
play a central role in the global economy due to their extensive use in
=== PAGE 12 ===
12
international transactions, foreign exchange reserves and financial
markets. Their importance lies in several key aspects: as a store of
value, where the world's central banks hold substantial reserves in USD
and EUR to stabilize their own currencies and manage economic crises;
as a medium of exchange, as the dollar and euro are widely used for
international trade, facilitating exchanges between countries; and as a
unit of account, as many financial contracts, including international
loans, are denominated in USD or EUR, reinforcing their central role.
Thus, the choice of USD/XXX enables a straightforward analysis about the
effect of US currency on trade balance.
The use of the Real Effective Exchange Rate (REER) is justified since it
adjusts for inflation differentials and trade structures with various partners, hence
reflecting the logic of Purchasing Power Parity (PPP) but going beyond it by
addressing its failure to account for shifts in trade flows and bilateral relationships,
as discussed by Mwange et al. (2022). The REER is a key variable for
macroeconomic analysis in open economies and essential for interpreting the
movement of exchange rates and their consequences on economic policy. A
careful measurement of the REER, using current weights, is required for a better
understanding of exchange rates and also their consequences on economic
policy (BAYOUMI et al., 2005). This role of the REER is related to its ability to
provide crucial information for policymaking, by deeper understanding of
exchange rate movements and their effects in the world economy, particularly
during crises (BAYOUMI et al., 2005).
Therefore, the combined use of the REER exchange rate and the
USD/XXX allows for combining both sets of perspectives, which consider the
direct impact of the U.S. dollar and global competitiveness adjusted for inflation,
thereby improving the predictive power of the analyses to be performed.
2.3 Trade flows and sectoral sensitivity
The structure of bilateral trade flows is a prerequisite for examining the
sensitivity of various sectors to exchange rate changes. The detailed bilateral
trade data at the Harmonized System (HS) level allows us to determine where
imports and exports go, what their value, volume, weight and thus modeling
specific sectoral trade relations. This level of granularity enables the analysis on
how changes in exchange rates, particularly those of the dollar, affect volumes of
=== PAGE 13 ===
13
trade (GOPINATH et al., 2020). Thus, it is possible to determine areas where
trade is more vulnerable or stable in the face of exchange rate shocks.
2.3.1 Sectoral impacts of exchange rate fluctuations
Exchange rate fluctuations affect sectors and products differently, and are
considered an important determinant of the competitiveness of goods in
international markets (NTHEBE; MOSIKARI, 2025).
Considering sectoral specificity is fundamental for accurately estimating
the elasticity of export and import demand, especially at the macroeconomic
level. The specialized literature shows that the exchange rate has had
differentiated impacts on prices across various sectors (GODA et al., 2024).
2.3.2 Commodities vs. manufactured goods vs high technology
Within different product types, such as commodities, manufactured goods,
and high-tech items, response to the exchange rate vary, as these products have
a particular complexity in the global value chain, in the elasticity of demand, and
in their cost dynamics (GODA et al., 2024).
Commodities, for example, are sensitive to exchange rate fluctuations, as
they reflect international prices which, in turn, respond directly to variations in the
dollar. These factors include production costs and the loss of export
competitiveness in a strong currency environment, automatically affecting export
incentives and domestic supply in an immediate way (OLIVEIRA et al., 2023).
Product complexity and the degree of technological intensity play important roles
in determining the sensitivity of sectors to exchange rate fluctuations, with low-
complexity sectors in developing countries exhibiting the greatest elasticities in
relation to variations in the real effective exchange rate. This is because less
sophisticated sectors generally have more flexible production costs and are much
more dependent on relative prices to maintain their competitiveness in the
international market (GODA et al., 2024).
=== PAGE 14 ===
14
From an opposite point of view, BERGIN and CORSETTI (2020) claim that
industries with differentiated goods, with nominal rigidity and costly entry, are less
reactive to the exchange rate than members of industries producing very
substitutable goods. This sensitivity is reduced by the nature of imperfect
competition character and a dependence on preconditions at the stable
macroeconomic level.
2.4 Macroeconomic control variables
The Gross Domestic Product (GDP) and the GDP per capita are among
the most commonly employed macroeconomic variables for the modeling of trade
flows as they track economic development level and the supply and demand
capacity of countries. The GDP per capita, which is GDP divided by population,
is especially important for measuring wealth disparities between countries and
how these might relate to trade patterns. As noted by Wahab (2024), this variable
helps explain how similarity, or dissimilarity, in factor endowments can affect
bilateral trade patterns, although it presents potential bias problems, such as the
accuracy of population data and the inherent limitations of official statistical data.
Another fundamental variable, conceptually linked to the exchange rate as
a dynamic condition, is inflation, which exerts a significant influence on the
international competitiveness of products and on the trade balance. For a
comprehensive analysis, the examination of the exchange rate regime must also
consider external equilibrium in monetary policy, depending on the inflation
targeting regime, since flexible rates are effective in correcting external
imbalances. Consequently, exchange rate flexibility, a distinctive characteristic of
a floating exchange rate regime, can lead to inflationary instability, which, in turn,
influences the trade balance (NASIR; VO, 2020).
Fluctuations in the nominal exchange rate also disrupt international
negotiations and directly influence production costs and the competitiveness of
tradable goods. While depreciation can increase imports of inputs but also make
exporting companies more competitive, exchange rate appreciation can result in
lower production costs, reducing import prices of inputs (SANTORO; ROLIM,
2023).
=== PAGE 15 ===
15
Financial and economic crises, which tend to accompany sharp exchange
rate movements and financial market turbulence, are also expected to affect trade
flows through changes in the supply of and demand for goods and services
(ARÉVALO et al., 2022).
2.5 Historical events
The global economy shapes trade flows and exchange-rate patterns as
booms and busts change investment, consumption, and internationalization. As
mentioned in SPRING-RAGAIN (2024, p. 2):
Global economic cycles are characterized by alternating phases of
expansion and recession, influenced by a multitude of economic,
political and social factors. These cycles are often punctuated by major
financial crises, such as the Asian crisis of 1997, the global financial
crisis of 2008, and more recently, the economic turbulence induced by
the COVID-19 pandemic.
2.6 Institutional events
The United States, Canada, and Mexico are home to one of the world's
largest free trade areas, initially called the North American Free Trade Agreement
(NAFTA), established in 1994, which opened trade and reduced barriers to
investment among the three countries. In 2020, NAFTA was replaced by the
United States-Mexico-Canada Agreement (USMCA), which preserves regional
integration and introduces appropriate structural changes, modernizes trade
rules, and includes new regulations (HIGUERA-COTA et al., 2023).
On the other hand, Brazil has long resisted comprehensive trade
agreements like the USMCA with the U.S., opting instead to strengthen regional
blocs such as MERCOSUR and refusing accession agreements modeled on the
U.S. Although the United States wished to expand its agreements with Brazil, the
South American country's policy did not align with the requirements of
"competitive liberalization" proposed in pacts like the USMCA; instead, Brazil
focused on seeking agreements with other Latin American nations (KAT, 2022).

2.7 Other determinants of trade flows
Other factors, beyond those previously mentioned, also impact trade
patterns to a great extent. A country’s population is one of the most important
factors because it indicates market size and potential import demand. The larger
the country, the more it can extend its trading network; here countries vary in their
market size (SELLAMI et al., 2024). Consequently, a nation's economic capacity,
indicated by Gross Domestic Product and national income per capita, crucially
affects its involvement in global trade. A strong GDP reflects robust production
conditions, which significantly impacts both import competitiveness and the level
of exports, while a high per capita income implies greater purchasing power
among people, thus promoting demand for higher value-added products and
improving both the quantity and quality of national export goods (LI et al., 2024).
Geographical distance is another undeniable determinant of trade, as it
represents a natural obstacle that results in transportation and time costs, which
ultimately impact the viability of economic relations at an international level. This
phenomenon, known as the Gravity Model, has several references in literature,
according to which trade between two countries is directly proportional to the size
of their economies, measured through variables such as GDP and population,
and inversely proportional to the distance that separates them (CONTRERAS,
2022). Related to this topic is logistics infrastructure, which includes the quality
and efficiency of ports, airports, highways, and railways. Efficient infrastructure
reduces costs and distances, favoring the rapid movement of goods, with a
consequent increase in commercial activities. On the other hand, a scarcity of
logistics factors can lead to higher transportation costs and inhibit commercial
transactions globally (MAO et al., 2023).
Equally important, or even more so, are the connections between states
that create a diplomatic environment conducive to trade. Currently, negotiations
no longer address only tariff reductions, but also the migration of people, capital
and knowledge, as well as cooperation (CARRILLO-MALDONADO; FLORES,
2024). In this sense, conflicts that block these flows can occur. It can also happen
that cultural factors, such as similar religious beliefs or a common language,
associated with certain countries, result in lower trade costs, combined with
=== PAGE 17 ===
17
greater trust between partners and, therefore, weakening barriers to entry in
trade. A similar culture can correct information asymmetries and influence
consumer preferences, thus stimulating trade in goods and services. In some
situations, cultural issues are more important for the trade of goods than the
quality of the products themselves, especially in goods emotionally linked to
cultural aspects (COSTA et al., 2023).
Finally, Global Value Chains (GVCs) are characterized as an international
network in which production stages are fragmented and a combination of
domestic and imported intermediate inputs is used to produce final goods and
services. Countries specialize in different activities, supplying intermediate inputs
that are used to produce both for export and for the domestic market (GAULIER
et al., 2020). Contemporary trade patterns are profoundly affected by increasing
global integration and all the phenomena associated with it, such as productive
globalization. In particular, the production of advanced high-technology goods
through international specialization and the fragmentation of production stages
has intensified trade flows and increased interdependence among participating
economies (AKMAN et al., 2020).
2.8 Time series forecasting models
The goal of time series analysis is to model and predict the behavior of
sequential data, as well as to discover temporal dependencies or patterns. The
conventional approach, reflected by econometric models such as ARIMA,
assumes that the problem is linear and that the data are stationary so that
statistical patterns remain stable over time and structural relationships can be
modeled. Preprocessing techniques, such as differencing, are used to make time
series data stationary (ABIR et al., 2024).
Time series analysis is considered basic in the understanding of economic
aspects, particularly regarding international trade and networks. It allows the
analysis of data at regular intervals in order to identify trends, cycles and patterns
that will help to explain the evolution of commercial behavior and the effects of
macro events, providing information on past activities, economical dynamics and
future performance (YAZAWA, 2023). In this sense, the ability to analyze traffic
=== PAGE 18 ===
18
aims to detect and predict these common temporal patterns in order to make
strategic decisions at the levels of international trade and finance (ARÉVALO et
al., 2022).
2.8.1 ARIMA model
AutoRegressive Integrated Moving Average (ARIMA) model proposed by
Box and Jenkins in the 1970s, is regarded as one of the classic statistical tools
for time series analysis and forecasting applications, especially in the finance field
(LIN et al., 2020). The model is particularly suitable for the series which have
linear trend, stationarity and no seasonality or can be transformed to satisfy these
requirements (HE et al., 2020).
This model has three main parameters: “p,” which is the order of
autoregression, “d” as the degree of differencing, such that stationarity is
reached, and “q”, as the moving average order, that allows capturing internal
dependencies or removing trends or seasonality in time series (CHATTERJEE et
al., 2024).
2.8.2 Smoothing and decomposition models
Exponential smoothing models are used for forecasting time series, using
local averaging with weighting of past data. In this case, the data have an
exponentially diminishing weight as they get older; indeed, any such model will
tend to give more importance to more recent events than historical data. This
approach is especially favorable when analyzing series with trends and
seasonality since exponential smoothing can be modified to accurately model
these aspects (SHEN et al., 2021).
Time series decomposition differs by dissecting the data into its structural
components, e.g., trend, seasonality, cycle, and error, eliminating or minimizing
noise, making the time series more suitable for forecasting. This decomposition
allows each part to be modeled separately and then recombined to produce a
final forecast (HE et al., 2020).
=== PAGE 19 ===
19
For the case at hand, such models are instrumental for deciphering
complex interactions in economic and financial systems, reporting dynamic
responses to shocks and their effect durations (ARÉVALO et al., 2022).
2.8.3 Stationarity tests
Stationarity is a fundamental subject when applying time-series models,
such as ARIMA. The differencing (parameter "d") is used where data demonstrate
non-stationarity to transform the series to make it conducive for ARIMA fitting
(CHATTERJEE et al., 2024). For the adequacy of time series models, formal tests
are performed, such as Dickey-Fuller test, to check the unit root and order of
integration process of the series that served as basis for further analysis, such as
co-integration (ARÉVALO et al., 2022).
Non-stationarity of time series, such as trend and seasonality, severely
damages the accuracy of models like ARIMA. Hence, the removal of non-
stationarity is an important prerequisite for the application of several time-series
models like ARIMA, and its elimination or alleviation has been pursued in
advanced approaches to improve forecasting performance (LIN et al., 2020).
2.9 Machine learning in forecasting
Recent advances in machine learning have made possible the
construction of more resilient and flexible models to forecast time series, which
are capable of dealing with non-linearity and complexity that characterize several
economic phenomena, surpassing the shortcomings of traditional statistical
approaches (CHINN et al., 2023). Machine learning algorithms, like Random
Forest and LightGBM, are chosen for this study due to their ability to capture non-
linearity and complex relationships within the data without imposing stringent
prior assumptions on data distribution or stationarity conditions (CHATTERJEE
et al., 2024).
While Random Forest and LightGBM were not specifically developed for
capturing temporal dependency, they are extensively used in time series
prediction tasks, due to their flexible ability to model complex relationships and
=== PAGE 20 ===
20
handle high dimensional data. In these applications, time can be combined by
feature engineering using the calendar information such as year and month as
well as transformation based on the series itself (ZHANG; JIANG, 2025).
2.9.1 Random Forest
Random Forest is one of the most versatile and widely used machine
learning algorithms for handling complex data. It is non-parametric, that is, it can
deal with different types of responses and predictors over scales and
distributions, making it appropriate for high-dimensional setting and complex
data. Its power becomes evident from the scale invariance of its frequency-based
functions and the fact that its prediction error remains stable even with an
increase in the number of trees (HU; SZYMCZAK, 2023). Hence it helps mitigate
the overfitting problem present in isolated decision trees since this model works
by creating multiple decision trees at training and outputting the class that is the
mode of classes (classification) or mean prediction result (regression) of the
individual trees (CHATTERJEE et al., 2024). This ensemble learning technique
by combining output from many trees reduces variance and bias, maximizing
predictive accuracy (LING; WANG, 2024).
What is more, Random Forest has great practical advantages for
applications in economics and business. It is moderately resistant to abnormal
points, can cope with multicollinearity between the predictors as well, and is able
to generate variable importance measure which further improve applied
interpretation. These traits can make the Random Forest a good choice for
balancing accuracy and interpretability on empirical scenarios (SALMAN et al.,
2024).
Nevertheless, it is important to recognize some of the methodological
weaknesses in the model that were raised by SELLAMI et al. (2024, p. 4):
However, while Random Forest models are well suited for analyzing trade

can hinder their accuracy in predicting future trade flows over time. Furthermore,

2.9.2 LightGBM
Otherwise, LightGBM is a gradient boosting framework that shows
efficiency and accuracy on large data sets while also being optimized for both
training and memory usages (CHATTERJEE; et al.; 2024). Its architecture can
make use of training acceleration techniques and reduce memory usage, which
would make it convenient for high-dimensional sparse scenarios. Such innovation
significantly contributes to model efficiency when carrying out machine learning
duties (GUO et al., 2024).
Gradient boosting models have been designed to be efficient, and it is
considered as an efficient tool for a machine learning problem where computation
is a concern and data sizes are very large though some models may behave
slowly under huge data size (ENKHBAYAR, ŚLEPACZUK, 2025).
In real-life scenarios, incomplete data is common, and as demonstrated in
studies, techniques such as oversampling with LightGBM manage this data and
address class imbalance (GOPINATH et al., 2021).
While it comes with the advantages of LightGBM, we can acknowledge
that exactly interpreting LightGBM still is a difficult task due to its naturally high
complexity in tree boosting models. Thus, although the model may result in highly
accurate predictions, it may do this less transparently than simple models, which
is pertinent when explainability is vital (GOPINATH et al., 2021).
Further, the models like LightGBM are also overly sensitive to overfitting,
which requires precise adjustments of numerous hyperparameters and careful
regularization. The optimization of these hyperparameters, which can be very
computationally demanding, is crucial for avoiding overfitting and promoting the
model learning to generalize well (SAIED et al., 2013).
Compared with some other models, which can be so complex that they are
not interpretable, LightGBM is usually in a good place on this trade-off. This

model is known for its high accuracy as well as its interpretability, making it an

2.9.3 Comparison between traditional approaches and ML
The main difference between machine learning models, like LightGBM and
Random Forest, is that they are intrinsically capable of modeling non-linear
relationships or complex interactions in the data. Unlike econometric approaches
that usually impose strong assumptions on the properties and distribution of data,
machine learning models rely much less on premises and adjust more easily to
changing data patterns (SELLAMI et al., 2024). This flexibility makes them
particularly appropriate for the prediction of complex phenomena, like exchange
rates and flows trade, overcoming the constraints and difficulties inherent in
traditional methods that do not always reflect the dynamics underlying data (GUO
et al., 2024).
Therefore, a main difference between machine learning and econometric
models is that, while in the former the data speaks for itself regarding patterns, in
the latter there is a need to make hypotheses based on predetermined model
specifications (SILVA et al., 2024).
On the other hand, in terms of data interpretation, available machine
learning models are consistently described as “black boxes”, making it difficult to
grasp the justification for their predictions compared with traditional econometric
models that are a more transparent and built on explicit economic theory
(ABOUZAID; BOUSSEDRA, 2025).
2.9.4 Hyperparameters and optimization
Although the "black box" nature of machine learning models presents
challenges, interpretability techniques, e.g., SHAP-based methods, are starting
to close this divide. These methods, inspired by cooperative game theory, allow
for more interpretable examination of how variables influence predictions of the
complex modeling task by decomposing predictions into feature contributions and

thereby expose non-linear structures and interactions that might have been
hidden otherwise (ZHANG; JIANG, 2025). SHAP is known to be very powerful in
understanding the importance of each input feature in a model's prediction about
a certain case, and in providing an index representing the contribution of each
variable to the output value of the model. This capability to assess the relative
importance of each feature for a given prediction improves interpretability and
trust in machine learning models, even for highly complex models (GUO et al.,
2024).
The use of these methods provides an opportunity for policymakers not
only to follow high-fidelity forecast predictions but also to understand the causal
reasons behind such forecasts, thus making better and more informed strategic
decisions (ZHANG; JIANG, 2025).
However, identifying the essential hyperparameters for model
performance is a major challenge in which slight changes can result in large
differences in predictive accuracy (ZHANG; JIANG, 2025).
Thus, the comparative application of Random Forest and LightGBM in this

3 METHODOLOGY
In this research, the Cross-Industry Standard Process for Data Mining
(CRISP-DM) was used to predict the impacts of changes in exchange rates on
trade flows between United States and Canada, Mexico, and Brazil. This decision
was motivated by the need to connect with macroeconomic database, time series
data and predictive models.
The methodology of CRISP-DM is widely recognized as one of the most
commonly used methodologies in data mining projects, which organizes the
project into well-defined phases. That structure guarantees the quality and
transparency of the models at all stages, from business understanding to
deployment, enabling the generation of insights and implementation analytical
solutions (TRIPATHI et al., 2021).
The process consists of six phases that will be the focus of this chapter:
Business Understanding, Data Understanding, Data Preparation, Modeling,
Evaluation and Deployment (WIRTH; HIPP, 2000).
3.1 Business understanding
This is the first phase of the CRISP-DM methodology, where the project's
goals are set and the required actions are determined. At this stage, requirements
are transformed to a definition of the data mining problem and project plans
defining activities for several subsequent steps (SHIMAOKA et al., 2024).
This stage also covers enlightenment in the economic and institutional
environment, identification of external forces like exchange rate volatility, global
macroeconomic shock and structural asymmetry. These factors are very
important because they affect both analytical models and the interpretation of the
results, and they should be included in policy making and whenever we evaluate
global impact (NGALAWA; KUTU, 2017).
Also, at this stage, the project’s success criteria are defined. These criteria
will be used as the comparison standard for assessing both the models’ outcomes
and the practical usefulness of the forecasts produced by them (MANDOLINI et
al., 2024).

3.4 Modeling
The process flow for Modeling, as an immediate continuation of Data
Preparation, is next after preparing and engineering features, as outlined in the
CRISP-DM methodology. At this stage, techniques like statistical tests and
machine learning algorithms are executed iteratively to construct, select and
refine predictive models used to detect patterns in the data (CHOWDHURY et
al., 2024; MA et al., 2024).
In this paper, we will utilize and compare both econometric models like
ARIMA and machine learning models such as Random Forest and LightGBM to
take advantage of their time series forecasting ability.
3.4.1 General overview of the modeling approach
In general, the modeling approach in time series prediction problems
involves a sequence of steps. Firstly, (i) the definition and preparation of features
for assembling a supervised model, including variable transformation and data
cleaning. Right away, (ii) finding a prediction window is essential to the predictive
goal of the model. The (iii) division of the data into training and test sets is a step
that aims at assessing how well the model's performance on unseen data,
simulating a real-world context. Afterwards, comes the phase of (iv) model tuning,
=== PAGE 36 ===
36
whereby hyper-parameters are adjusted, e.g., using of cross-validation ensuring
that the derived models generalize well. Then, the (v) comparative evaluation is
compared in terms of its strategy impact and economic contributions, based on
metrics. Finally, (vi) explainability analysis of the model is appreciated to gain
higher performance and confidence (SILVA; TIMO, 2022).
Avoiding data leakage, the so-called "contamination zone" effect, is
important because inconsistency between the training and testing phases can
lead to erroneous assessments of model performance (REMIL et al., 2024).
3.4.2 Data format for modeling
The way to define time series models as inputs is varied, and ARIMA
models are widely used for univariate time series. Furthermore, models like
SARIMA, a variation of ARIMA, can also contain mechanisms to deal with non-
stationary data, such as trends and seasonality (PORTO, 2022).
For Random Forest and LightGBM, both of which are models based on
decision trees, the data input can be multivariate and does not require time series
stationarity. This flexibility allows them to more easily incorporate several
macroeconomic lagged variables (BOTZ et al., 2025). This flexibility enables the
incorporation of features derived through feature engineering, e.g., lags, which
add time delays to allow models to capture short and medium-term
dependencies, moving averages, and calendar features, all of which are pivotal
for capturing temporal and seasonal dynamics in the data (ZANOTTI, 2025).
3.4.3 ARIMA model: definition and justification
The AutoRegressive Integrated Moving Average (ARIMA) model has been
used as a benchmark method for time series analysis, such as financial and
economic time series. Its approach involves the removal of non-stationarity,
primarily through differentiation, to enable modeling the time series (LIN et al.,
2020).
=== PAGE 37 ===
37
Its parameterization (p, d, q) models autoregressive dependencies (p), the
level of differentiation required to remove trends (d), and the moving average
component (q) (RIZVI, 2024). ARIMA has been employed as a fundamental
baseline model for comparative forecasting performance studies, and in certain
applications, it can even emerge as the best approach for short-term forecasts,
serving as an effective baseline for the evaluation of more complex models
(BOTZ et al., 2025).
3.4.4 Specific procedures for ARIMA
For each single-variable time series, the standard modeling practice starts
with a stationarity test, for which the Augmented Dickey-Fuller (ADF) test is often
applied, to determine the order of differentiation (d) of the series. Examination of
the Autocorrelation Function (ACF) and Partial Autocorrelation Function (PACF)
plots are useful to suggest the appropriate parameters (p) and (q) for the model.
These visual analyses allow for the reduction of the parameter search space
(REICHERT; SOUZA, 2020).
The final model selection is then performed using information criteria such
as the Akaike Information Criterion (AIC) and the Bayesian Information Criterion
(BIC). The goal is to identify the minimum values of these criteria to detect the
most parsimonious and best-fitted models. Finally, a rigorous diagnostic analysis
of the residuals is crucial to confirm the absence of heteroscedasticity and
autocorrelation, which are White Noise properties, thereby validating the model’s
assumptions and ensuring its adequacy (REICHERT; SOUZA, 2020).
The backtesting process for models such as ARIMA can occur
retrospectively using a sliding window approach. Models are trained on historical
data up to a point in time (t0), and predictions are then generated for the following
period (t0+1). This process is repeated sequentially, thereby dividing the time
series into several training and testing windows, which allows for the comparison
between predictions and actual values and the evaluation of the model's
predictive stability over time (BOTZ et al., 2025).

38
3.4.5 Preparation and modeling for Random Forest
Random Forest is implemented using an ensemble approach which
aggregates multiple decision trees over the supervised matrix, as it is applied in
prediction tasks (ABIR et al., 2025).
Unlike ARIMA, it performs well for non-linear relationships and is capable
of capturing complex interactions between predictors without assuming the
underlying data to be stationary (CHATTERJEE et al., 2024).
The aforementioned algorithm is tolerant of various data types and less
sensitive to outliers and multicollinearity, which is an advantage for dealing with
macroeconomic and financial data (LING; WANG, 2024; SALMAN et al., 2024).
3.4.6 Preparation and modeling for LightGBM
Similar to Random Forest, Light Gradient Boosting Machine (LightGBM) is
a powerful time series forecasting algorithm. This specific algorithm is known for
its speed and accuracy, often demonstrating superior performance in processing
large, high-dimensional datasets (ZANOTTI, 2025). It is also based on a
supervised matrix, but it requires caution in the optimization of hyperparameters
and the treatment of missing values (MORTEZANEJAD; WANG, 2025).
This model is particularly strong when it comes to dealing with complex
interactions between features and uses a histogram-based method to construct
trees. By binning the training data, this approach significantly decreases memory
consumption and speeds up training. For this reason, LightGBM is highly efficient
for data with many features and can effectively learn complex patterns,
outperforming other algorithms in both accuracy and execution time
(MORTEZANEJAD; WANG, 2025).
3.4.7 Precautions with machine learning models
Nevertheless, the employment of well-tuned machine learning models for
financial market prediction, though appealing, should be treated with caution due
to uncertainty about how well such models can generalize across diverse market
=== PAGE 39 ===
39
conditions and the need for thorough sensitivity analyses to validate model
stability (WANG; XIE, 2024).
Besides, the risk of overfitting should also be considered to ensure
effectiveness in real-world scenarios and durability of the algorithms across
different historical periods (WANG; XIE, 2024).
3.4.8 Temporal validation and evaluation
Temporal validation is key in time series analysis because it maintains the
temporal order of the data, avoids information leakage from future to past, and
ensures model evaluation under realistic forecasting settings (MORTEZANEJAD;
WANG, 2025).
Techniques like the rolling window and the expanding window are
employed. With the rolling window, the model is trained on a fixed period and this
window slides over time. The expanding window, however, adds new data to the
training set in each iteration, expanding the available historical data horizon
(ZANOTTI, 2025).
3.4.9 Training, validation and test split
To minimize problems of improper evaluation and over-generalization,
time series should be strictly split according to chronological order (KHAN et al.,
2024).
Typically, approximately 80% of the series is employed for model training,
while the remaining 20% is used for prediction and performance comparison. This
preserves the temporal structure and prevents access to future data during
training, thereby mimicking the natural flow of time and consequently avoiding
data leakage (KHAN et al., 2024; SAFAROV et al., 2025). This approach is
adopted in this study.
ARIMA models are univariate and will therefore straightforwardly process
one time series. Data manipulation and preparation, including the differencing
operation to achieve stationarity, are done intrinsically at the level of the series
=== PAGE 40 ===
40
itself; that is, no external shifts or alterations are made for target vectors, as in
other approaches (BOTZ et al., 2025).
Because of this structure, in addition to training and validation, this
approach employs backtesting strategies based on sliding window methods. This
is often referred to as time series cross-validation (TS-CV), which enables
iterative evaluation of model performance, thereby ensuring that both training and
test data preserve chronological order during validations (ABIR et al., 2024).
3.4.10 Hyperparameter optimization
In Machine Learning model development, hyperparameter tuning is
essential for maximizing performance, with various techniques being applied
(VRANJEŠ; NIGGEMANN, 2024).
The most common methodologies include: Grid Search, an exhaustive
search approach that evaluates all predefined configurations; Random Search,
which selects configurations randomly and is often more efficient, finding similar
performance while shortening the computational time; and Optuna, which
involves training a probabilistic model relating hyperparameters to the model's
performance metrics. Each methodology has its unique characteristics that
impact the efficiency and scope of the hyperparameter search space (RASCHKA
et al., 2020; LING; WANG, 2024).
Given the modeling approach, as part of the CRISP-DM process flow, we
move to Evaluation, which allows models to be compared based on their
accuracy and practicality of use.
3.5 Evaluation
In the CRISP-DM methodology, the evaluation phase is essential to verify
that the models developed not only meet the business objectives previously
defined in the Business Understanding phase, but are also sufficiently tuned for
deployment (SATRI et al., 2023).
=== PAGE 41 ===
41
Model validation is achieved by scrutinizing the performance,
interpretability, and robustness metrics of a model to confirm its credibility and
utility in decision-making (SUDJIANTO; ZHANG, 2024).
3.5.1 Statistical and economic evaluation metrics
To compare the models, the assessment of predictive accuracy and
practical relevance requires the use of robust statistical metrics, such as the Root
Mean Squared Error (RMSE), the Mean Absolute Error (MAE), and the Mean
Absolute Percentage Error (MAPE), all of which are frequently employed to
gauge prediction accuracy (AYITEY et al., 2023).
Besides statistical metrics, economic metrics such as simulated
investment profit, net and gross financial returns, operating costs, and risk ratios,
e.g., Sharpe and Sortino ratios, are crucial for evaluating the practical applicability
and financial viability of the models in real-world market scenarios, thereby
providing a comprehensive view of performance (MACHADO et al., 2020).
Performance measures are necessary for the purpose of comparing
machine learning models to each other and to traditional approaches like ARIMA
to determine which model is most effective for a specific application. These
evaluations are essential to quantify the value added by complex models over
baseline methods and also validate the dependability and generalizability of the
model (UDDIN et al., 2025).
3.5.2 Interpretation and explainability of models
Although machine learning-based models may provide superior
performance, their interpretability is often difficult to achieve, particularly with
financial applications where the clarity of the decision-making process and
transparency are a necessity for decision-making and regulatory compliance
(SUDJIANTO; ZHANG, 2024).
To circumvent this opacity, interpretability methods such as feature
importance analysis and SHAP-based explainers are applied. These methods
=== PAGE 42 ===
42
elucidate the contribution of each input variable to the model's predictions, which
facilitates a deeper understanding of its decision-making process and the
individual impact of features on the outcomes (GUO et al., 2024).
This enables policymakers and financial analysts to understand not only
“what” is predicted, but also “why” certain predictions are generated. Overcoming
the opacity of “black boxes” is crucial for cultivating trust and the adoption of these
predictive tools (ABOUZAID; BOUSSEDRA, 2025). In addition, the model’s
interpretability is crucial for users such as policymakers and investors to
understand its forecasting logic and make informed decisions. This indirectly
validates the coherence of its relationships with economic indicators (KHAN et
al., 2024).
Moreover, explainability is crucial to identify potential biases in models and
to ensure compliance with regulatory requirements, particularly in sensitive
sectors, e.g., the finance sector (SUDJIANTO; ZHANG, 2024). This ability to
account for the working of the model, including a detailed explanation of how
economic variables influence outcomes, is also essential for supporting its
predictive ability and helps ensure that it reflects a sensible understanding of the
underlying economic principles (KHAN et al., 2024).
3.5.3 Robustness tests and sensitivity analysis
Robustness tests and sensitivity analysis are the methods used to check
the validity and performance of a model across a variety of market conditions
(SUDJIANTO; ZHANG, 2024).
These procedures are also essential in providing systematic assessments
of model stability, including analysis under various market conditions, adaptability
to simulated shocks and extreme events, and resilience during high-volatility
periods. Such investigations could also include diagnostic tests for the stability of
the coefficients and adaptability to meaningful changes in the data (ABOUZAID;
BOUSSEDRA, 2025).
This process confirms that the models are dependable, coherent, and
congruent with the purposes of this research. Hence, the evaluation provides the
=== PAGE 43 ===
43
necessary basis for the informed choice of the most adequate model, guiding the
study's conclusion and its potential applications.
3.6. Deployment
Deployment is the phase in the CRISP-DM methodology which includes
all activities to be performed in an operational environment, including packaging
the code and integrating it into a system such that the organization can use it. It
includes establishing real-time infrastructure and continually monitoring quality
and effectiveness in a rapidly changing environment (SHIMAOKA et al., 2024).
3.6.1 Purpose of implementation in predictive modeling projects
This is the final and ongoing phase of the model lifecycle, whose aim is
not only to ensure that developed models work technically but also to bring
continuous value to the business (TRIPATHI et al., 2021). This involves
converting predictions and insights into accessible and usable artifacts for end
users, such as dashboards for analysts, visual notifications on existing systems,
periodic reports to managers, or automated notifications to operations, ensuring
that information is delivered in a manner that is understandable and actionable
for various stakeholders (PODDAR et al., 2024).
3.6.2 Requirements for predictive model implementation
Conceptually, implementation requires observing minimum requirements
on various fronts. This ranges from high-quality data, considering update
frequency, formats, and sources, to an adequate infrastructure to ensure
scalability and robustness, and well-established processes, such as data
ingestion and validation pipelines, to ensure the model's reliability and continuous
maintenance (TRIPATHI et al., 2021). For this implementation to be effective, it
requires a multidisciplinary process that's tightly integrated with adopted
operational systems, along with intuitive and useful user interfaces. This effort
must encompass making predictions available for consultation, the precise
=== PAGE 44 ===
44
identification of responsible parties for every stage, and covers everything from
updating the databases to disseminating the results (PODDAR et al., 2024).
3.6.3 Structure of a theoretical prediction pipeline
A theoretical prediction pipeline should involve a structured process that
includes, but is not restricted to: Data ingestion and preprocessing, Feature
engineering, Model building, Model evaluation, and the Persistence and
publication of results, including standard components such as Extract, Transform,
Load (ETL) and API deployment (RASCHKA et al., 2020; PODDAR et al., 2024).
Every step must apply best practices that enable the traceability and
auditability of the process, such as mandatory log storage and change
management, which provide greater transparency by allowing the monitoring of
changes and model use (NOGARE; SILVEIRA, 2024). These logs record
performance metrics, missing data, execution times and error events, and can be
stored in systems (NOGARE; SILVEIRA, 2024).
Data versioning is fundamental for the reproducibility of experiments,
reinforcing the importance of recording detailed information at every stage of the
model lifecycle (PEIKERT; BRANDMAIER, 2021).
3.6.4 Operational data preparation
During implementation, the data preparation has an operational aspect
that goes beyond what has been already done with transformations in the
research setting. That is, there is a necessity to translate training queries into
reusable pipelines that deliver data to the model, which may involve infrastructure
for collecting data periodically or in real-time (PODDAR et al., 2024).
Preprocessing code, including unit tests, must automate basic checks like
the validation of data completeness and consistency (PODDAR et al., 2024).
Such validations may be carried out using specialized frameworks like Great
Expectations or Deequ, which offer the possibility to define and document
=== PAGE 45 ===
45
expectations regarding the data, guaranteeing its integrity, accuracy, and
consistency (DIAS et al., 2020).
3.6.5 Forecast generation: operational issues (Batch vs. Near Real-Time)
Predictions can be made in a variety of methods. Some of these strategies
employ historical data and real-time alert information to anticipate the likelihood
of future incident events, which leads to a Near-Real-Time operation mode where
execution is performed upon new incoming data (REMIL et al., 2024).
In contrast, other approaches face with the issue of incident prediction and
are usually made as predictions within a specific time interval, which suggests an
operation mode closer to Batch or periodic executions (REMIL et al., 2024).
In the present work, the most feasible theoretical alternative would be
Batch generation with a known horizon (t1, t6 and t12), along with validation
routines that maintain consistency of the outputs before publication.
3.6.6 Results integration in the business context
The results integration must be planned based on the practical utility of
forecasts for each target audience (SILVA; TIMO, 2022). From a conceptual
perspective, the operationalization of a forecasting system is an issue related to
clarify definitions about clear output formats, aggregation levels, and delivery
mechanisms to ensure that the model results are usable by end-users (PODDAR
et al., 2024). Tables and graphs summarizing forecasts are among the intended
outputs of this study, which are aggregated by country, sector, and forecast
horizon, and are made available through periodic reports and interactive
dashboards.
User-related technical guidelines and specifications for results are of
critical importance, as the effectiveness of forecasting software, in turn, depends
on human perception and acceptance, particularly for non-experts. An opaque
system can be rejected. Therefore, it is fundamental that the user can assess and
interpret the forecasts for subsequent tasks (STROMER et al., 2024).
=== PAGE 46 ===
46
3.6.7 Theoretical monitoring and maintenance framework
The monitoring stage is essential to guarantee that models are not simply
working technically, but also consistently delivering business value by adapting
and optimizing based on new information and contextual changes through regular
recalibration, periodic retraining, and continuous learning with new data
(SUDJIANTO; ZHANG, 2024).
Triggers for the model reevaluation, but are not limited to, significant error
increases evidenced by performance metrics, for example, degradation in RMSE,
drift detection in data or concept that surfaces as altered input data distribution,
and which might result in a compromised validity of the model, and changes
parallel with a changing environment or market can bear direct implications on
how data behave over time (SUDJIANTO; ZHANG, 2024).
3.6.8 Documentation, versioning, and reproducibility
Documentation and versioning are essential for traceability and
transparency throughout the entire model lifecycle, allowing for the understanding
of its operation and the audit of its decisions. Version control, for example,
records changes to files over time so specific versions can be recalled later or
revert the entire project to a previous state (PEIKERT; BRANDMAIER, 2021).
In relation to code versioning, systems such as Git and collaborative
platforms like GitHub are used (SEMMELROCK et al., 2024). Additionally,
versioning of data, artifacts and parameters is fundamental for reproducibility,
being supported by domain-specific tools like DVC and MLflow, which are
responsible for managing several artifacts associated with model performance
and validation.
The possibility to easily revert any changes made is a key factor to foster
experimentation and innovation, as it reduces the perceived risk involved in
adopting new approaches (STROMER et al., 2024).

3.6.9 Academic reporting
Upon the completion of the implementation, an extensive academic report
should be compiled to provide clear understanding for the entire process carried
out, formalizing results and methodologies and insights achieved, and ensuring
that generated knowledge is captured and disseminated for future applications
and research (PODDAR et al., 2024).
In the context of this study, a paper in continuation of this monograph
should include the applied CRISP-DM methodology, Random Forest and
LightGBM model architecture and a detailed comparative analysis with ARIMA
model results. To enable reproducibility, this report must include technical
appendices, such as code segments, samples of transformed data, and
information about execution environments.
A consideration of the limitations of the study, opportunities for future
research and the ethical and regulatory implications of utilizing advanced
predictive models in an economic context must also be included (SUDJIANTO;
ZHANG, 2024).

5 APPENDIX
5.1 Interview script
In order to validate the results obtained and minimize bias in the research,
open-ended questions were designed for two distinct profiles: professionals with
extensive knowledge on foreign trade and international business, and IT
professionals. The questions had been worded in an unbiased and open way
specifically not to induce responses so that the participants could communicate
generally and in detail.
The script aims at capturing these opinions to add a qualitative validation
of the models and methodological choices used in this work. The questions
address the practical applicability of the forecasts, the relevance of the factors
chosen, the acceptance of the models, and the consistency of the decisions
adopted in the project.
5.1.1 Questionnaire for foreign trade / international business professionals
How do machine learning forecasting models, e.g., Random Forest,
LightGBM, compare with traditional time-series models such as ARIMA for the
task of forecasting in foreign trade? What do you perceive are the strengths or
limitations of each approach?
Are economic factors considered in the models (exchange rate, inflation,
GDP, and other variables) relevant to predict international trade indicators? Are
there other factors that you might be inclined to include?
How might the outcomes anticipated by these models be valuable in
decision-making within your field? Can you provide some examples of practical
applications, e.g., export/import planning or demand analysis, where these
forecasts would have an impact?
What is your sense of the interpretability and trust that managers and
analysts might have in these predictive models? Would you trust a classic
=== PAGE 50 ===
50
statistical model (ARIMA) or a machine learning model (Random Forest,
LightGBM) more? Why?
What other fail-safe checks or additional safety measures would you
recommend to make sure that such models do not introduce unwanted biases in
the analysis of foreign trade?
5.1.2 Questionnaire for technology professionals
Technically speaking, what are the main challenges and benefits of
utilizing machine learning models (Random Forest, LightGBM etc.) for
forecasting economic or business indicators compared to traditional time series
models such as ARIMA? And which approach has proven more effective
according to your experience?
In the sense of data and variables, do you think the chosen economic
factors, e.g., macroeconomic variables, are suitable to feed predictive models?
Do you think other types of data should be included, e.g., sectoral indicators,
market data, or alternative sources, to make it more accurate?
What explainability or visualization strategies do you consider important
for communicating the results of complex models to a non-technical audience?
How can transparency be ensured in the modeling process so that the model's
decisions are understandable?
How do you feel the technology community, or IT companies, generally
perceives the acceptance of ML-based predictive models compared to traditional
statistical approaches? What metrics or technical evidence would be necessary
to convince stakeholders to adopt an ML model?
Have you deployed predictions by your model, either ARIMA or ML, into a
live system/application? What was that experience like? Many of the descriptors
shared for this were a variation of "I can work with an acceptable model." What
made the model acceptable, e.g., ease of integration, production performance,
and user confidence?
